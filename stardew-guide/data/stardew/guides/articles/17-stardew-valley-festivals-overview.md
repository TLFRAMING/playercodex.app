# Stardew Valley Festivals Guide: Every Festival and What to Watch For

Stardew Valley festivals are special calendar events that interrupt the normal farm routine. Some are contests, some are social gatherings, some are shops, and some are multi-day events where time keeps moving normally.

This guide lists every major festival, explains what the event is for, and gives practical notes so you know whether to bring money, an item, inventory space, fishing gear, or simply enough time to attend.

## Festival Rules To Know First

Most festivals are optional, but they can still affect your day. For many daytime festivals, homes and shops are locked for the whole day, even if you skip the event. When you leave most standard festivals, the game sends you back to the farm later at night.

There are important exceptions. The Desert Festival, Trout Derby, SquidFest, and Night Market behave more like open events: normal time continues, and shops and homes are not shut down in the same way. Treat those as days where you can still plan normal chores, travel, and errands around the event.

If a festival starts before evening, do your farm chores first. If the event has a shop or reward, empty your backpack and bring gold. If the event asks for an item, choose it before leaving the farm.

## Spring Festivals

### Egg Festival - Spring 13

The Egg Festival is the first major festival most players see. It takes place in Pelican Town and is built around the Egg Hunt, where you collect colored eggs against a short real-time timer.

What it is for: the festival introduces event contests and gives you access to a seasonal shop. The most important early-game purchase is usually Strawberry Seeds, because they can still produce harvests before Summer if planted soon after the festival.

What to watch for: bring money before entering town. If you spend all your gold before Spring 13, you may miss the chance to buy strawberries. The Egg Hunt reward is nice, but the bigger long-term value is often the seed shop.

### Desert Festival - Spring 15-17

The Desert Festival is a multi-day event in the Desert. It includes special shops, event currency, challenges, betting, and rotating opportunities.

What it is for: this is a later-access festival built around Desert activity rather than a single town cutscene. It gives players a reason to spend several days interacting with the Desert once that area is available.

What to watch for: time continues during this festival, so do not treat it like a paused event day. Plan farm chores, travel, food, and inventory before going. If your save has not unlocked reliable Desert access yet, this festival may not matter much in your first year.

### Flower Dance - Spring 24

The Flower Dance takes place in Cindersap Forest. Its main feature is asking an eligible bachelor or bachelorette to dance.

What it is for: this is a social festival. Dancing with a partner gives a friendship boost, but the partner must already like you enough to accept.

What to watch for: new players often arrive expecting a guaranteed dance and get rejected. You need at least four hearts with an eligible partner before they agree. If you are not close to anyone yet, use the festival as a social moment and shop check rather than a failed objective.

## Summer Festivals

### Luau - Summer 11

The Luau takes place on the Beach and centers on the communal potluck soup. You contribute one item, and the Governor reacts to the quality and type of the ingredient.

What it is for: the Luau is a friendship event. A good soup result can help your relationship with many villagers at once, while a bad ingredient can hurt it.

What to watch for: choose your soup item carefully before leaving the farm. Do not toss in random low-quality forage just because it is available. If you do not have a strong ingredient, it is better to be cautious than to damage town-wide friendship.

### Trout Derby - Summer 20-21

The Trout Derby is a two-day fishing event in Cindersap Forest. You catch Rainbow Trout for a chance at Golden Tags, then exchange those tags for prizes.

What it is for: this is a fishing-focused reward event. It is useful if you enjoy fishing or want extra prizes without giving up the whole day to a locked festival.

What to watch for: time keeps moving and normal buildings are not broadly closed. Bring fishing supplies, clear inventory space, and treat it like a fishing day with an event reward layer. If your fishing skill is still rough, focus on practice and tags rather than perfect prize farming.

### Dance of the Moonlight Jellies - Summer 28

The Dance of the Moonlight Jellies happens late at night on the Beach. Villagers gather to watch the jellyfish migration.

What it is for: this is mostly an atmosphere and social festival. It marks the end of Summer more than it offers a progression reward.

What to watch for: because it happens at night on the last day of Summer, your real preparation is season change. Finish crop plans, clear fields if needed, and handle tomorrow's seed money before going. Do not let the event distract you from the Fall 1 reset.

## Fall Festivals

### Stardew Valley Fair - Fall 16

The Stardew Valley Fair takes place in Pelican Town. It includes minigames, Star Tokens, a prize shop, and the Grange Display contest where you show farm products.

What it is for: this is one of the most reward-focused festivals. It rewards a broad farm with good-quality items across different categories and gives you a chance to earn shop prizes through Star Tokens.

What to watch for: bring display items you are willing to carry around. After judging, remember to collect your Grange Display items before leaving. If you forget them, they are not gone forever, but recovering them later is unnecessary friction.

### Spirit's Eve - Fall 27

Spirit's Eve is the Halloween-style night festival in Pelican Town. It features a maze, a themed shop, and a special maze reward.

What it is for: this is an exploration festival. The main activity is finding your way through the maze and checking the seasonal shop.

What to watch for: it starts late, so you can still use most of the day before attending. Bring gold if you want decorations or shop items. The maze has a hidden path near the end, so slow down and inspect suspicious dead ends instead of assuming the obvious route is all there is.

## Winter Festivals

### Festival of Ice - Winter 8

The Festival of Ice takes place in Cindersap Forest. It includes ice sculptures, a small shop, and an Ice Fishing Contest.

What it is for: this is a contest festival. The fishing contest gives rewards for winning and helps turn Winter into more than a quiet farming season.

What to watch for: you are given a rod for the contest, but your fishing habits still matter. Cast quickly, keep failed catches from wasting too much time, and do not panic if you lose in Year 1. You can return in later years with better player skill.

### SquidFest - Winter 12-13

SquidFest is a two-day Beach fishing event focused on catching Squid. Each day has its own prize structure based on how many Squid you catch.

What it is for: this is a targeted fishing challenge with tiered rewards. It gives Winter fishing a clear short-term goal.

What to watch for: the counter resets for the second day, so treat Winter 12 and Winter 13 as separate attempts. Time continues normally and buildings are not broadly closed. Bring bait, tackle if available, food if needed, and enough inventory space for fish and prizes.

### Night Market - Winter 15-17

The Night Market is a three-night event on the Beach. It has shops, special services, the Mermaid Boat, and the submarine ride for deep-sea fishing.

What it is for: this is a shopping and activity festival. It is one of the most flexible events because it runs in the evening across multiple days.

What to watch for: normal time continues, so you can work during the day and visit later. Bring gold and inventory space. If you want deep-sea fishing, leave enough time for the submarine activity instead of arriving at the last moment.

### Feast of the Winter Star - Winter 25

The Feast of the Winter Star takes place in Pelican Town and centers on secret gift-giving. Mayor Lewis sends you a letter in advance naming your gift recipient.

What it is for: this is a friendship and gift-planning festival. You give a present to one assigned villager and receive one from another villager.

What to watch for: read the letter before the festival and prepare a gift for that exact villager. Do not wait until the morning of Winter 25 to check your inventory. If you are unsure what they like, use your safest verified gift option rather than guessing with a random item.

## Which Festivals Matter Most For Beginners?

If you are short on time, prioritize festivals that affect money, rewards, or friendship at scale.

The Egg Festival matters because Strawberry Seeds can shape Spring income. The Luau matters because one ingredient can affect many villagers. The Stardew Valley Fair matters because the Grange Display and Star Tokens can produce meaningful rewards. The Feast of the Winter Star matters because it asks you to prepare a specific gift.

Fishing festivals are useful if fishing is part of your save, but they are less important if you dislike fishing or lack supplies. Social and atmosphere festivals are still worth seeing once, but they should not derail farm stability.
